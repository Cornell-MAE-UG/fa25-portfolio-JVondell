<h2 id="client-pitch">Client Pitch</h2>

<p>
Current methods for removing spotted lanternflies (SLF) risk damaging grape plants or contaminating harvested grapes. Methods that require distinguishing between insects and plant material increase complexity, while direct-contact approaches can harm vines or leave residue on grapes. Chemical pesticides are also largely ineffective and may introduce additional risks.
</p>

<p>
Our team focused on developing a non-contact removal method that can be integrated into the harvesting process. The goal is to remove SLF effectively while minimizing damage to plants and reducing reliance on chemical treatments.
</p>

<h2 id="functional-prototype">Functional Prototype</h2>

<p>
The purpose of the functional prototype was to test whether a vacuum-based device could effectively remove SLF without damaging grape vines.
</p>

<p>
The prototype consists of a fan-driven suction system with a cone inlet, tube, and collection box that captures SLF using airflow. It is designed to be mounted on a harvester and remove insects before grapes are processed.
</p>

<h4>What We Tested</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Fan durability under continuous use</li>
  <li>Resistance to debris of varying sizes</li>
  <li>Structural support and load capacity</li>
</ul>

<h4>Results</h4>
<ul>
  <li>The fan operated successfully but the shaft showed warping and alignment issues</li>
  <li>Small debris passed through easily, but larger debris caused blade damage</li>
  <li>The mounting system successfully supported over 500g without failure</li>
</ul>

<h4>Prototype Evaluation & Next Steps</h4>
<p>
The prototype demonstrates that a vacuum-based approach is feasible, but requires material and durability improvements before real-world use. Future iterations should include stronger materials, improved alignment, and a debris filtering system.
</p>

<h2 id="client-report">Client Report</h2>

<p>
Our final solution is a suction-based device designed to remove SLF from grapevines without physical contact. It uses a motor-driven impeller to generate localized airflow, pulling insects into a collection chamber while minimizing risk to grapes and vines.
</p>

<p>
The final prototype includes an impeller, circular cone, aluminum mesh filter, motor casing, rechargeable battery, and mounting system for integration with tractors or harvesters. This design allows for efficient, repeatable removal while fitting into existing agricultural workflows.
</p>

<h4>Key Testing Results</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Operated continuously for 30 minutes with no performance drop</li>
  <li>No internal damage from debris between 1–5 cm and 1–10 g</li>
  <li>Supported 500g load without structural failure</li>
  <li>Effective suction range averaged ~1.7 cm, indicating need for stronger suction</li>
</ul>

<h4>Recommendation & Next Steps</h4>
<p>
The design is promising but not yet ready for field deployment. Future work should focus on increasing suction strength, reducing nozzle size, and improving coverage of the vine. Potential solutions include actuator integration, multiple devices, or scaling up the motor and impeller system.
</p>
